The Truth that Killed

Mid-century stoicism in Georgi Markov's written-for-the-radio essays

„Проституцията“ (Prostitution) 

Introduction

In this chapter titled "Проституцията" ("Prostitution"), Markov explores the complex issue of prostitution in socialist Bulgaria, highlighting both the official denial and the reality behind it. Markov details a meeting between writers and officers from the Ministry of Internal Affairs, tasked with combating various societal "vices," including prostitution. The ministry's official stance is that prostitution doesn't exist in a socialist country; instead, they categorize women engaged in such activities as having "immoral behaviour." Despite this claim, the ministry actively monitors and attempts to rehabilitate about 300 women in Sofia who are involved in these activities.

The chapter reveals the contradictions and hypocrisies within the system. Markov and his fellow writers meet several women, each representing different aspects of this hidden world. Their stories reflect broader societal issues, such as economic hardship, lack of opportunities, and moral degradation, all against the backdrop of a socialist regime that is officially committed to eradicating such problems. Markov suggests that these women are not isolated cases but symptomatic of deeper societal corruption. While the state tries to "reform" them, the regime itself is complicit in fostering the conditions that lead to their marginalization, as prostitution becomes intertwined with political manipulation, black-market activities, and the state's own covert operations. 

Semantics and Word Choice

Markov’s word choice and tone reveal a deep-seated critique of the socialist regime's denial of its own complicity in social degradation. His use of irony, juxtaposition of official and colloquial language, and emotive descriptions of the women convey a mix of frustration, pity, and moral outrage, emphasizing the tragic hypocrisy of the state's moral posturing.

Official Language vs. Reality: Phrases like “неморално поведение” (“immoral behaviour”) and “антиобществено явление” (“anti-social phenomenon”) reflect the state’s sanitized, bureaucratic approach to prostitution. By using these official terms, Markov underscores the regime's desire to distance itself from the moral decay it refuses to acknowledge openly. The dispassionate tone of these phrases contrasts sharply with the grim reality of the women’s lives, creating a sense of hypocrisy in the government’s efforts. Markov’s ironic tone is evident in how he presents the state’s language. For instance, he writes: “Официално... в страната няма проституция” (“Officially... there is no prostitution in the country”). The word “Официално” (“officially”) drips with sarcasm, as Markov immediately reveals the contradiction between this claim and the actual existence of prostitution. By placing this formal statement next to the gritty, lived reality, he mocks the state’s failure to confront its own societal issues.
 
Demeaning Descriptions of Women: The portrayal of the women reflects a spectrum of hopelessness and defiance. Words like “възпълна грозничка жена” (“plump, ugly woman”) and “плитко самочувствие” (“shallow confidence”) convey pity, while more dehumanizing descriptions, such as “уплашено добиче” (“frightened animal”), suggest how the women are perceived as less than human within this system. These descriptions evoke a sense of their degraded status, highlighting the emotional and social isolation they endure. The harsh language used to describe them mirrors the systemic neglect and exploitation they suffer under. Markov uses vivid, colloquial expressions to cut through the façade of socialist morality. The phrase “като мечка на панаир” (“like a bear at a fair”) conjures the image of these women being paraded for the amusement of the powerful, reinforcing their objectification and lack of agency. Similarly, the exclamation “Че кой не е проститутка в тая държава?!” (“Who isn’t a prostitute in this country?!”) from one of the women captures the deep cynicism that permeates the narrative. This blunt and provocative language aligns with Markov’s broader critique of a society where moral corruption runs from the top down, rendering the official distinctions between good and bad behaviour absurd.

Symbolism of Decay: The repeated use of words such as "разпад" (“decay”), "загубено" (“lost”), and "нищо няма значение" (“nothing matters”) evoke a nihilistic atmosphere, symbolizing the broader collapse of moral and social structures in Bulgaria. These expressions of futility and resignation suggest that the decay of society is not just physical or economic but deeply moral, reflected in both the personal and public lives of its citizens.

Sentence Structure

Markov’s use of both long and short sentences serves to deepen the emotional impact of the passage. The longer, more detailed sentences reflect the state's cold detachment, while the shorter, more abrupt sentences capture the raw emotions of the women and underscore the hypocrisy and contradictions inherent in the regime's policies. This varied sentence structure contributes to the overall mood of cynicism, despair, and moral decay that pervades the passage.

At moments, Markov uses longer sentences that seem to flow like a stream of consciousness, particularly when characters express frustration or hopelessness. For example, when the third woman bursts into the room, her speech is delivered in long, breathless sentences: “Какво ме водите като мечка на панаир!... Добре ли ми е, зле ли ми е, това си е моя лична работа!” (Why are you dragging me around like a bear at a fair!... Whether I’m doing well or not is none of your business!). The length of the sentences mirrors her emotional outburst and sense of being overwhelmed, creating a fast-paced rhythm that conveys her anger and frustration. The rush of words reflects her lack of control over her situation, adding to the chaotic mood of the passage.

The varied sentence lengths affect both the pacing and mood of the passage. Longer sentences slow down the narrative, forcing the reader to linger on the details of the women’s exploitation and the state's hypocritical stance. This slower pace creates a reflective, almost methodical mood, as if Markov is laying out the facts of the case against the regime. In contrast, short sentences speed up the pacing, heightening the emotional tension and creating a sense of urgency or despair. These shifts in pacing mirror the emotional highs and lows of the women’s lives, from moments of resignation to bursts of defiance.
 

Long Sentences

Markov uses long, multi-clause sentences when providing detailed descriptions of the characters, their circumstances, or the state's hypocritical policies. For example: “Полковникът бавно ни обяснява, че в неговия отдел има картотека на около 300 момичета от София, чиято дейност може да се смята за антиобществено явление...” Here, the long sentence mimics the bureaucratic tone of the state’s explanations, drawing out the description to emphasize the absurdity of the situation. The slow, methodical pace reflects the detached, official language of the state, which contrasts with the grim reality of the lives being discussed. This longer structure also allows Markov to pack in irony, as the formal tone of the sentence highlights the state's superficial approach to controlling prostitution.
 

Short Sentences

Short, blunt sentences are often used to deliver moments of sharp emotional impact or to underscore a bitter truth. For instance: “Тя отказва да седне.” (She refuses to sit.) The simplicity and brevity of this sentence signal a moment of defiance, conveying the woman’s resistance without unnecessary elaboration. The abruptness of the short sentences punctuates the longer, more descriptive passages, creating a jarring effect that grabs the reader's attention and heightens the emotional intensity. It also adds to the sense of disconnection between the characters and the writers observing them.

Another example is: “Нищо. Нищо няма значение!” (Nothing. Nothing matters!) The repetition and brevity of this line reflect the nihilistic despair of the character, giving the sentence an immediate emotional punch. This use of short sentences intensifies the mood of hopelessness, making the reader pause and feel the weight of the woman’s emotional state.
 

Juxtaposition 

Markov frequently alternates between long and short sentences, which creates a contrast that enhances the tension and reflects the contradictions within the narrative. The longer sentences provide the reader with detailed, almost clinical information, while the short sentences cut through that formality with raw emotion or blunt truths. This contrast helps to underscore the gap between the official narrative of moral reform and the harsh, lived reality of the women being discussed.

For example, the passage describing the meeting with the first woman includes a mix of long sentences explaining her situation and the state's efforts, followed by short, sharp responses from the woman: “Е, щастлива ли си сега? – О, другарю полковник, толкова съм Ви благодарна...” (Well, are you happy now? – Oh, comrade colonel, I’m so grateful to you...)

The shift from a formal question to her rehearsed, almost mechanical response is made more impactful by the change in sentence length. It highlights the disingenuousness of the interaction, making the woman’s hollow gratitude feel like a performance rather than a sincere expression of thanks. 

Figurative Language

The metaphors, similes, and imagery in this passage serve to deepen the reader’s understanding of the key themes in The Truth That Killed: the moral decay of the socialist regime, its exploitation of women, and the pervasive sense of despair and futility that characterizes life under such a system. These literary devices also provide insight into the narrator’s internal experience, reflecting his growing disillusionment with the regime’s hypocrisy and its failure to address the real problems facing its citizens.

A "социалистически сводник" (“socialist pimp”): Perhaps the most striking metaphor in the passage is when Markov compares the socialist regime to a “pimp,” suggesting that the state itself is complicit in the exploitation of these women. By framing the regime as a “социалистически сводник” (socialist pimp), Markov reveals the corrupt underbelly of a system that, while publicly denouncing prostitution, privately benefits from it. This metaphor underscores one of the central themes of the book: the state’s hypocrisy and moral corruption. Just as a pimp profit from the bodies of others, the regime profits from the exploitation of women, whether through the use of prostitutes for intelligence operations or as a byproduct of the state’s neglect and inequality

“като мечка на панаир” (“like a bear at a fair”): This simile is used to describe one of the women angrily confronting the police, comparing herself to a bear being paraded at a fair. The image suggests dehumanization and exploitation; just as a bear is trained and forced to perform for public entertainment, these women are being showcased for the amusement or curiosity of the police and writers. The simile reflects the narrator's recognition of the system's cruelty—how the women are stripped of their dignity and treated as mere spectacles rather than human beings. This dehumanization speaks to a broader theme in the book: the state’s exploitation of individuals while maintaining a pretence of moral superiority.

Decay and nihilism: Several characters, particularly the women, express a sense of futility and loss. Phrases like “Нищо няма значение!” (“Nothing matters!”) and descriptions of life as “загубено” (“lost”) evoke imagery of moral and emotional decay. This sense of nihilism reflects the broader collapse of moral and social structures in Bulgaria, where individuals, particularly women in this situation, feel powerless and without purpose. The narrator’s internal experience of disillusionment is mirrored in this imagery, as he grapples with the realization that the regime is incapable of addressing the root causes of these problems. Instead, the state’s denial and superficial interventions only deepen the sense of despair.

Control and manipulation: The entire scene of the police gathering and interrogating the women evokes imagery of control and manipulation. The women are described as moving through a system that tracks, catalogues, and attempts to "rehabilitate" them. The sterile language of the police and the bureaucratic processes, such as the "картотека" (card catalogue) of 300 women, highlights the mechanistic and impersonal nature of the regime’s approach. This imagery of control contrasts sharply with the chaotic and unpredictable nature of the women’s lives, symbolizing the state's futile attempts to impose order on something it neither understands nor genuinely cares about. The narrator’s detailed observation of this system reflects his growing awareness of the state's ineffectiveness and the emptiness of its moral posturing.

Prostitution as a “паразит” (“parasite”): One of the women rejects the state’s label of her as a "parasite," arguing that she takes nothing for free and works hard for everything she gets. The metaphor of prostitution as parasitism highlights how the regime views these women as leeches on society, draining its resources without contributing. However, the woman’s rejection of this label complicates the metaphor, revealing that she is, in fact, a product of the very system that seeks to marginalize her. This imagery exposes the state’s failure to take responsibility for the social conditions it creates—conditions that force women into these roles. For the narrator, this moment may reinforce his internal conflict, as he begins to see that the state’s portrayal of these women is not only inaccurate but deeply unjust.

Hopelessness and defiance: The passage includes vivid descriptions of the women’s emotional responses to their situations, ranging from resignation to outright defiance. For example, one woman refuses to sit, standing as though she is resisting both the physical and metaphorical pressure to conform or submit. Another woman describes how nothing matters and that she has no faith in the system or her future. These moments of defiance, juxtaposed with their ultimate powerlessness, create an atmosphere of tension and hopelessness. For the narrator, these scenes likely evoke a sense of helplessness and frustration as he witnesses individuals who, like him, are trapped within an inescapable system of exploitation. 

Theoretical insights

Post-Colonial Theory

Through the lens of post-colonial theory, this passage from The Truth That Killed can be read as a critique of the socialist state’s internalized systems of control, drawing parallels to colonial mechanisms of power and exploitation. Although Bulgaria was not a traditional colony, Markov’s depiction of the regime's handling of prostitution reflects the broader dynamics of domination, marginalization, and exploitation typically associated with colonial rule. The socialist state, like a colonial power, exerts its control over marginalized women, stripping them of agency and reducing them to mere objects of surveillance and manipulation. The metaphor of the regime as a “социалистически сводник” (socialist pimp) parallels the way colonial powers extracted resources from colonized populations. Here, women’s bodies become a site of state exploitation, manipulated for political gain, intelligence work, and as a means of exerting control over the populace. This metaphor critiques the state's claim to moral and ideological superiority, revealing its deep complicity in the systemic exploitation of vulnerable individuals, much like colonial regimes exploiting local populations under the guise of civilization or progress. 

Memory Studies

From the perspective of memory studies, Markov’s passage reflects the tension between personal memory and official state narratives in socialist Bulgaria. The women’s stories, particularly their expressions of despair, frustration, and defiance, represent lived experiences that challenge the state’s officially constructed narrative of moral purity and social order. The regime's attempts to rehabilitate these women or erase their existence from public consciousness are acts of silencing and rewriting history, in line with state-controlled memory practices. The contrast between the state’s formal denial of prostitution and the women’s lived realities underscores the struggle between individual memory and collective memory as shaped by authoritarian power. The imagery and testimonies in this passage reveal how personal memories of suffering and marginalization are not only erased but repurposed by the state for political aims, as in the use of women for intelligence work. Markov’s narrative itself can be seen as an act of resistance, capturing and preserving the marginalized memories of individuals that would otherwise be suppressed by official discourse, thereby challenging the hegemonic memory of the socialist regime.

Affective Theory

The emotional responses of the women—ranging from anger, resignation, and defiance to nihilism—are central to understanding how the state’s power is felt and internalized at the bodily level. The imagery of dehumanization, such as the simile comparing a woman to a “bear at a fair,” conveys a deep emotional violence inflicted by the regime, reducing the women to mere objects of spectacle. The intense emotional states of the women, particularly their expressions of hopelessness and rage, reveal how the state's systemic exploitation impacts their mental and emotional well-being, creating a sense of alienation and loss of self. The narrator’s own emotional response to these encounters—his growing disillusionment and frustration—mirrors the affective atmosphere of the passage, as he witnesses the state’s indifference to the suffering it causes. In this context, the passage highlights the affective dimensions of power and control, showing how emotions are both a site of resistance and a tool of oppression in the interactions between the state and its citizens.

Narrative Voice

The narrator’s voice in this passage from The Truth That Killed is both subjective and highly engaged, reflecting a deep emotional and intellectual investment in the events described. The first-person perspective, combined with the narrator's sharp observations and ironic tone, shapes the reader's understanding of the socialist regime’s failings and the broader social realities in Bulgaria. The narrator’s subjectivity is evident in his critical stance toward the state’s policies, particularly in how he juxtaposes the official denial of prostitution with the harsh realities faced by the women. This personal viewpoint invites the reader to question the regime’s authority and to sympathize with the marginalized individuals depicted in the story.

Although the narrator is clearly subjective, his reliability is reinforced by his detailed descriptions and nuanced reflections on the contradictions between state rhetoric and lived experience. For instance, the narrator provides concrete examples of the regime's hypocrisy—such as the description of women being used by State Security as informants—while simultaneously expressing personal disillusionment and moral outrage. His ironic and sometimes cynical tone, particularly when describing the state’s superficial campaigns to address "immoral behaviour," suggests that he has insider knowledge of the deeper corruption at play. This positions the narrator as both a participant and a critic, lending credibility to his perspective even as he acknowledges the emotional weight of the encounters.

The narrative perspective profoundly shapes the reader’s understanding by framing the events through a lens of moral and political critique. The narrator’s focus on the women’s experiences, contrasted with the state’s mechanical, bureaucratic responses, emphasizes the human cost of the regime’s failures. His voice oscillates between a detached observer and a deeply engaged moral commentator, which draws the reader into the complexities of the situation while making it clear where the narrator’s sympathies lie. Thus, while the narrator is subjective, his insights are valuable for exposing the moral contradictions of the regime, making his perspective a crucial guide for the reader in interpreting the events and the broader themes of power and exploitation.
