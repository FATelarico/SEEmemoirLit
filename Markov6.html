The Truth that Killed

Mid-century stoicism in Georgi Markov's written-for-the-radio essays

„Паспортната одисея“ (Passport Odyssey)

Introduction

The chapter titled "Паспортната одисея" (Passport Odyssey) revolves around the character Pencho Danchev, a humble carpenter at a tram depot in Sofia, who embarks on a bureaucratic odyssey to secure a passport to visit his cousin in Belgrade. The story begins with Pencho’s frustration when his passport application is rejected without any clear reason. As he navigates the oppressive and unwelcoming government office, he reflects on his lifelong fear of authority, compounded by an acute sense of helplessness. Despite his law-abiding and hardworking nature, Pencho finds himself caught in a system where arbitrary restrictions and bureaucratic indifference reign. He becomes increasingly determined to challenge the system, though he harbours no real hope of success.

Over the course of several months, the quest for a passport consumes Pencho's life. He discovers that many of his peers have faced similar struggles, realizing that the issue is not personal but systemic. Pencho is repeatedly advised to seek a “връзка” (connection) to navigate the corrupt system, but as a modest worker with no influential contacts, this option feels impossible. Eventually, after enlisting the help of a distant relative with government connections, Pencho is granted a passport, though the process leaves him disillusioned and emotionally drained. The once-exciting prospect of visiting his cousin is now tainted by the humiliation and futility he experienced. The chapter closes on a bleak note, with Pencho musing on the pervasive corruption and injustice that define everyday life in communist Bulgaria. 

Semantics and Word Choice

Through specific word choices and phrases, Markov conveys a sense of suffocating oppression, where ordinary citizens like Pencho are rendered powerless against a dehumanizing, corrupt system. The language emphasizes the absurdity, cruelty, and senselessness of the bureaucratic state, reflecting Markov's deep critique of the communist regime's arbitrary control over individual lives. The passage brims with emotions of frustration, resignation, and a muted anger, all pointing to the moral and existential exhaustion inflicted on people by the state's pervasive authority.

"Одисея" (Odyssey): The title itself invokes the idea of a long, arduous journey fraught with challenges, mirroring Pencho’s bureaucratic struggle for a simple passport. The term "odyssey" conveys the sense that this is not just an administrative process but an epic battle against a monolithic, labyrinthine system. It elevates the trivial act of applying for a passport into a Herculean task, suggesting the author’s critique of the state's unnecessary complexity and absurdity.

"Отвратителна сграда" (Disgusting building) and "груба, безмилостна атмосфера" (coarse, merciless atmosphere): These descriptions of the government office reflect the hostile and dehumanizing nature of the bureaucratic system. Words like "disgusting" and "merciless" emphasize the author’s negative attitude toward the institution, evoking a physical and moral revulsion. The atmosphere is not merely indifferent but actively "coarse" and "merciless," hinting at the cruelty embedded in the state's functioning.

"Свиждане в Централния затвор" (visiting someone in the Central Prison): By comparing the experience of visiting a passport office to that of entering a prison, Markov suggests that the state views its citizens as criminals, stripping them of agency and dignity. This metaphor aligns the bureaucratic process with punishment, highlighting the oppressive control the state wields over individuals.

"Простия работник" (simple worker) and "смаже" (crush): Pencho’s self-description as a "simple worker" juxtaposed with the threat of being "crushed" by the state conveys the immense power imbalance between the individual and the regime. The verb "смаже" (crush) suggests violent force, implying that the state’s power is not just overwhelming but brutal, capable of destroying the lives of ordinary people with ease. This word choice intensifies the theme of helplessness.

"Струва ми се" (It seems to me): This phrase, appearing as Pencho reflects on his desire to travel, conveys doubt and resignation. It reveals the futility he feels as an individual trapped in a system where even reasonable desires are seen as absurd or impossible. This language expresses the weariness and hopelessness of someone conditioned to expect rejection and failure.

"Нелепо и безсмислено" (absurd and meaningless): Pencho’s growing sense of the absurdity of his quest is captured in this phrase, reflecting the author’s broader critique of a system that renders even the most basic human freedoms, like travel, into absurd and unattainable privileges. The repetition of words connoting futility ("absurd," "meaningless") enhances the sense of disillusionment.

Sentence Structure

Markov’s use of both long and short sentences shapes the pacing and mood of the passage in significant ways. Long sentences slow down the narrative, mirroring Pencho’s entrapment in a never-ending bureaucratic process and amplifying the feeling of futility. Short sentences, on the other hand, intensify moments of emotional outburst, signalling frustration and highlighting the stark realities of a dehumanizing system. Together, these variations in sentence structure not only affect the passage's pacing but also deepen the portrayal of the absurdity and cruelty of the communist state, as experienced by ordinary citizens like Pencho Danchev.
 

Long Sentences

Markov often employs long, complex sentences when describing Pencho's internal monologue and the bureaucratic process he faces. For example, Pencho's thoughts on why he deserves a passport—his clean record, his hard work, and his loyalty to the state—are presented in an extended, flowing sentence: “Защо да не мога да пътувам аз, който не съм престъпник, който съм работил цял живот като вол за благото на тая държава...” (Why shouldn't I be able to travel, who am not a criminal, who have worked all my life like an ox for the good of this country) This sentence continues as Pencho mentally lists all the reasons he believes should guarantee him a passport. The length and structure of the sentence mimic the mental exhaustion of someone desperately trying to justify a reasonable request. The piling on of clauses mirrors Pencho’s increasing frustration, as if he’s struggling to find a way through an impenetrable wall of state logic. The extended structure also suggests an internal dialogue, one where Pencho tries to convince himself of his own worth and rights, but in doing so, becomes caught in a web of justifications.

The long sentences also slow down the pacing, creating a sense of dragging time, much like Pencho’s bureaucratic ordeal. The length mirrors the endless waiting, the layers of paperwork, and the feeling that no matter how much time and effort Pencho invests, he remains trapped. This slow, laborious pacing amplifies the sense of futility and resignation, with the bureaucratic process seeming never-ending.
 

Short Sentences

In contrast, when Markov shifts to shorter, more abrupt sentences, the tone becomes sharper, heightening tension and a sense of finality. For instance, when Pencho reaches the climax of his frustration, he expresses himself more simply and directly: "Пуснете ме, дето се казва, свят да видя! Защо да не ида?" (Let me, as they say, see the world! Why should I not go?)

This shift to short sentences enhances the emotional rawness of the moment. Pencho’s earlier justifications have evaporated, replaced by a desperate plea. The brevity of these sentences mirrors the collapse of his hopes, stripping his argument down to its most basic form, representing an almost childlike demand for fairness. The abruptness signals the breakdown of his patience and highlights the futility of further discussion, reinforcing the sense that reasoned arguments are pointless in this situation.

Similarly, when Markov describes the oppressive environment of the government office, short phrases create an atmosphere of coldness and detachment: "Слънце никога не наднича в това помещение." (The sun never peeps into this room.) This short, declarative sentence paints the office as a bleak, lifeless space. The concise structure mirrors the rigid, unyielding nature of the bureaucracy Pencho faces. There's no elaboration, no warmth; just a stark statement of fact, reflecting the emotional sterility of the setting.
 

Juxtaposition 

The alternation between long and short sentences also creates a rhythmic contrast that enhances the passage's emotional impact. As Pencho’s thoughts and emotions ebb and flow between hopeful justification and despairing resignation, the sentence structure follows suit. Longer sentences convey the mental strain of navigating a complex and illogical system, while shorter sentences capture moments of emotional collapse, emphasizing feelings of powerlessness.

This structural contrast mirrors Pencho's oscillation between hope and hopelessness, pushing the reader through a similarly fluctuating emotional experience. As the passage progresses, the increasing presence of shorter sentences signals the breakdown of Pencho’s resolve, as his detailed justifications give way to raw frustration and exhaustion. 

Figurative Language

The metaphors, similes, and imagery reveal Pencho's internal experience of helplessness, fear, and frustration. The monstrous and prison-like qualities of the state apparatus underscore the overwhelming power imbalance between the individual and the bureaucracy. Pencho's journey for a passport becomes a symbol of the broader struggle for personal autonomy in a system designed to suppress and control. The imagery of darkness, decay, and voicelessness reinforces the themes of oppression, absurdity, and the dehumanizing nature of the communist regime. Ultimately, these literary devices deepen the portrayal of the state's crushing influence on its citizens, revealing how even simple desires are turned into unattainable dreams.

"Уста на чудовището" (the mouth of the monster): Markov compares the state bureaucracy to a monstrous entity, with Pencho Danchev feeling as though he is stepping into the "mouth of the monster" as he enters the passport office. This metaphor transforms the government institution into something terrifying and predatory. It suggests that the bureaucracy is not merely indifferent but actively hostile, capable of consuming and destroying individuals. The metaphor highlights Pencho’s fear and the sense of being devoured by a faceless, brutal system. This image also reflects the broader theme of the state as an all-powerful, inhuman force that consumes its citizens, stripping them of agency and individuality. Pencho, a "simple worker," feels insignificant and vulnerable, facing a regime that holds absolute power over him.

"Паспортът за чужбина става за Пенчо Данчев това, което е тиквата за Душко Добродушков" (The passport to travel abroad became for Pencho Danchev what the pumpkin was for Dusko Dobrodushkov): This metaphor links Pencho's quest for a passport to a fairy tale-like scenario where an object (the passport) assumes a magical, transformative role, much like the pumpkin in folklore stories that serves as a vehicle to freedom or adventure. However, the reference to the pumpkin also suggests an element of absurdity or futility, as Pencho’s dream of obtaining a passport, though as innocent and simple as a fairy tale desire, is met with unreasonable resistance from the system. The metaphor emphasizes the absurdity of Pencho’s situation and reinforces the theme of bureaucratic irrationality.

"Нелепо и безсмислено би било, ако искаше да го коронясат за император на Япония" (As absurd and meaningless as wanting to be crowned emperor of Japan): This simile highlights the sheer absurdity Pencho feels about his own quest for a passport. His desire to visit his cousin in Belgrade, something that should be simple and reasonable, is made to seem as unattainable and ridiculous as being crowned emperor of Japan. This simile reflects Pencho's growing sense of powerlessness and the irrationality of the bureaucratic system that treats his basic request as outlandish. It underscores the futility of individual desires in the face of a system that denies basic freedoms without reason.

Тази отвратителна сграда край Лъвовия мост" (This disgusting building near the Lion’s Bridge): The imagery of the passport office as a "disgusting building" evokes a sense of physical and moral decay. The location near the Lion's Bridge (an iconic place in Sofia’s city centre) adds a layer of irony, as the majestic imagery of lions contrasts with the filth and oppression that the building represents. The atmosphere inside the office, described as resembling a prison visitation room, adds to the claustrophobic and dehumanizing experience Pencho faces. This imagery portrays the state as an institution that imprisons its citizens psychologically, even when they are not physically behind bars.

"Слънце никога не наднича в това помещение" (The sun never peeks into this room): This image of a sunless room suggests an environment devoid of warmth, hope, or life. The absence of sunlight symbolizes the oppressive and lifeless nature of the bureaucracy, where human emotions and individual needs are ignored. The cold, shadowy atmosphere reflects the dark, uncaring face of the state, emphasizing how it suppresses any light or freedom. This imagery reinforces the theme of an all-powerful regime that casts its citizens into darkness and hopelessness.

"Лъвовете" (The lions): The recurring image of the lion statues on the Lion's Bridge takes on symbolic significance in the final scenes. When Pencho sees the lions with their mouths open but without tongues, the image represents voicelessness and impotence. The lions, traditionally symbols of strength and power, are rendered mute, much like the powerless citizens in the face of the state. This final image encapsulates Pencho’s realization that, despite all his efforts and the system’s promises, he remains voiceless and insignificant. It serves as a potent metaphor for the broader experience of living under a repressive regime where individuals have no say or control over their own lives.

Theoretical insights

Post-Colonial Theory

Although Passport Odissey is set within the context of a communist regime, it can be analysed through a post-colonial lens, particularly regarding power dynamics and internal colonization. The state apparatus, which controls and limits the freedom of movement, acts as a colonizing force over its own people. The passport becomes a symbol of the way the state exerts control, not just over external borders, but also over the bodies and identities of its citizens. Pencho’s helplessness in the face of bureaucratic barriers can be compared to the experiences of colonized individuals who face systemic oppression and the denial of basic human rights. The dehumanizing system mirrors the colonial structure where a central authority exerts domination and reduces individuals to mere subjects rather than full citizens. The metaphor of the "monster" that Pencho steps into reflects how the regime alienates and marginalizes its own people, like how colonial powers alienate native populations, stripping them of autonomy and dignity. 

Memory Studies

Through the lens of memory studies, Паспортната одисея explores the collective memory of life under communist Bulgaria. Pencho’s struggle for a passport taps into the broader cultural memory of repression, surveillance, and restricted freedom. The imagery of the oppressive bureaucracy, with its labyrinthine procedures and unyielding officials, evokes a shared experience of generations subjected to similar control. The repetition of procedures—submitting applications, waiting, and appealing to authority figures—reflects a form of institutional memory embedded in everyday life, where citizens learn to navigate systems designed to keep them in place. Memory here is also shaped by the trauma of being denied basic human rights, as reflected in Pencho’s resignation to never obtaining his passport. His eventual success is hollow, as the experience of systemic oppression has left an indelible scar on his psyche, showing how memory can be shaped by both personal and collective encounters with a repressive state.

Affective Theory

Affective theory offers insights into how emotions drive the narrative and shape the protagonist's experience. Pencho’s journey through the bureaucratic system is laden with feelings of frustration, helplessness, and anger. The state's control over his ability to travel stirs a complex affective response, oscillating between hope and despair. The oppressive environment of the passport office, described as cold and sunless, induces a feeling of dread and powerlessness, which infects Pencho’s emotional state. The lengthy, oppressive process also evokes a sense of emotional exhaustion, where even his eventual success feels meaningless. The reader, too, is drawn into this affective economy, feeling the frustration of Pencho’s repeated failures and the anti-climactic nature of his "victory." The use of metaphors like the "mouth of the monster" reflects the affective weight of the state, which induces fear and submission, rendering individuals like Pencho emotionally and physically small in the face of such overwhelming power. Affective theory, thus, illuminates how emotions like fear, frustration, and resignation are central to understanding the human experience of state control in the narrative.

Narrative Voice

In this chapter, the narrator adopts a subjective and highly empathetic voice that aligns closely with the internal thoughts and emotions of the protagonist, Pencho Danchev. While the narration is technically third-person, it operates with limited omniscience, giving readers intimate access to Pencho's mental and emotional landscape. This choice of narrative perspective shapes the reader's understanding of events in several important ways, presenting the bureaucratic system as an absurd, dehumanizing force while casting Pencho as a sympathetic figure caught in a web of oppression.

The narrator is highly subjective, often reflecting Pencho’s emotional states and framing the events through his perspective. For example, the language used to describe the passport office as a "disgusting building" with a "coarse, merciless atmosphere" clearly reflects Pencho's feelings of dread and fear. The narrative aligns with his sense of powerlessness, using metaphors like the "mouth of the monster" to communicate how threatening and overwhelming the bureaucracy feels to him. This subjectivity shapes the reader’s perception of the state as an all-encompassing, faceless antagonist, heightening the sense of injustice and irrationality in the system. As a result, the reader is encouraged to sympathize with Pencho and share in his frustration and disillusionment.

While the narrator provides a deeply personal and subjective view of Pencho’s experiences, the voice remains reliable in the sense that it accurately conveys Pencho's internal struggles and reactions to the oppressive system. However, the perspective is filtered through Pencho’s limited understanding of the broader political context, which may not offer a fully objective account of the events. For example, when Pencho questions why others can travel freely while he cannot, the narrator focuses on Pencho’s sense of confusion and injustice without necessarily providing a larger analysis of the political reasons behind the state's restrictions. This limited perspective shapes the reader’s understanding, making the system seem even more irrational and arbitrary because it withholds explanations that could potentially contextualize the state’s actions (however unjust those might be). Ultimately, the narrative perspective shapes the reader’s experience by presenting a clear emotional and psychological account of Pencho’s ordeal, but it also amplifies the sense of futility and helplessness by maintaining a narrow, subjective focus. The lack of broader insight into the regime’s motives mirrors Pencho's limited knowledge, reinforcing the idea that individuals under such a system are kept deliberately in the dark, unable to understand or change their circumstances.
