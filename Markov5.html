The Truth that Killed

Mid-century stoicism in Georgi Markov's written-for-the-radio essays

„Зад фасадата на 8-и март“ (Behind the Façade of the March 8th)

Introduction

The chapter titled "Зад фасадата на 8-и март" (Behind the Facade of March 8th) offers a critical reflection on the celebration of International Women's Day in socialist Bulgaria. The narrative begins with a vivid description of an enthusiastic celebration organized by women at a publishing house. There is a palpable sense of excitement, with women from all roles in the organization coming together to plan and perform a program for the event. Although the atmosphere is festive and the women's efforts are celebrated, the narrator expresses a deeper dissatisfaction with the hollow nature of this single day of recognition. He notes that the gestures of appreciation, such as small gifts, only mask the systemic neglect, exploitation, and oppression that Bulgarian women face throughout the year.

The chapter then transitions to a broader critique of the changes in the roles of Bulgarian women during the past 30 years. While the shift from traditional roles as housewives and mothers to active participation in the workforce might seem like progress, Markov argues that this transition has not truly liberated women. Instead, many women now bear the dual burden of maintaining their households and working demanding jobs, often in physically taxing or underpaid roles. Economic independence has, paradoxically, led to increased strain on women, and the ideal of equality remains far from realized. Through personal anecdotes and a critical tone, Markov highlights the deep disconnect between the state’s rhetoric of emancipation and the lived experiences of women, many of whom feel more exploited and undervalued than ever. The façade of gender equality, symbolized by the March 8th celebrations, contrasts starkly with the continued societal and economic pressures placed on women in socialist Bulgaria. 

Semantics and Word Choice

Markov’s word choice is imbued with irony and critique. The use of diminutive or belittling terms alongside stronger language highlighting exhaustion and dishonesty suggests both frustration with the facade of women’s liberation and sympathy for the burdens they carry. His language evokes a sense of injustice, where symbolic gestures mask deeper societal inequalities, and where the supposed emancipation of women has not led to true freedom, but rather to further exploitation.

Dismissing and patronising: The phrase "Празниците са за жените и за децата" ("Holidays are for women and children") is subtly dismissive. By equating women with children, the narrator hints at a patronizing attitude toward women. The implication is that women, like children, are indulged or entertained with symbolic gestures that mask their subordination in the rest of society. This infantilization of women is reinforced by the way the holiday is structured—a day of festivity, devoid of substantive change. With "съчинявам празните, дежурни фрази" ("I compose empty, routine phrases"), the word "празни" (empty) directly conveys the narrator’s recognition of the hollowness behind the words spoken during the celebration. The adjective "empty" suggests insincerity and disillusionment, implying that the praise for women is superficial and lacks genuine meaning. The word "дежурни" (routine) reinforces this notion, indicating that such statements are obligatory, not heartfelt, and part of a repetitive, meaningless ritual. The terms "дребен подарък," "признание" и "троха" ("small gift," "recognition," and "crumb") emphasize the trivial nature of the gestures made toward women. Similarly "дребен" (small) and "троха" (crumb) suggest that the acknowledgment given to women is minimal and insufficient. The word "признание" (recognition) is used ironically, as the "recognition" women receive is fleeting and symbolic rather than meaningful or transformative. In "колко дълбоко неискрено и нечестно е нашето, мъжкото отношение към българската жена" ("how deeply insincere and dishonest our, the male, attitude towards the Bulgarian woman is"), Markov uses the strong terms "неискрено" (insincere) and "нечестно" (dishonest) to express the profound hypocrisy of men’s attitudes toward women. These words convey anger and frustration with the societal norms that treat women with surface-level respect on March 8th, while fundamentally disregarding their worth throughout the year.

An impossible situation: The phrase "тихият, покорен глас на някогашната домакиня" ("the quiet, submissive voice of the former housewife") evokes an image of passivity and compliance. The use of "тихият" (quiet) and "покорен" (submissive) underscores the historical oppression of women, presenting them as voiceless and resigned to their inferior status. The contrast with the "new" voice of women who assert themselves indicates a shift, but Markov portrays this shift as incomplete and fraught with contradictions. Meanwhile, "свръх силите й," "свръхчовешка заетост" ("beyond her strength," "superhuman workload") , using the prefix "свръх-" (beyond, super-), emphasize the overwhelming demands placed on women. The hyperbolic language conveys exhaustion and strain, suggesting that the so-called liberation of women has actually placed unbearable burdens on them. This reflects Markov’s critique of the unrealistic expectations placed on women in socialist society. The word "героизъм" ("heroism") typically carries positive connotations of bravery and strength, but in this context, it is used to point out the unreasonable and unsustainable demands placed on women. The sarcastic undertone suggests that what is celebrated as "heroism" is actually a form of exploitation, where women are forced to endure hardships with little recognition or support.

Sentence Structure

As for the other chapters, the author’s use of long and short sentences in this passage shapes both the pacing and mood. Long sentences slow the pace, immersing the reader in the complexity of women’s lives and the burdens they face. Short sentences, by contrast, quicken the pace, offering moments of sharp critique and emphasizing the disillusionment with the supposed "freedom" women have achieved. Together, these sentence structures create a rhythm that reflects both the exhaustion of the women’s daily lives and the growing frustration with the superficiality of societal gestures like International Women’s Day.

.
 

Long Sentences

Markov frequently uses long sentences when describing the broader societal changes and the daily lives of women. For example:

"Самият факт, че с оглед да подпомага мизерния семеен бюджет обикновената българка трябваше да прибави към съществуващите семейни задължения и бремето на работата, службата или професията, без да получава никакви съществени облекчения, се оказа свръх силите й." (The very fact that, in order to support a meagre family budget, the ordinary Bulgarian woman had to add to her existing family obligations the burden of work, service or profession, without receiving any substantial relief, proved beyond her strength.")

This sentence is rich with clauses and ideas, reflecting the overwhelming burdens placed on women. The use of multiple clauses mirrors the complexity and weight of the many responsibilities women have to balance—family, work, and societal expectations. The sentence structure, with its dense layering of ideas, conveys a sense of suffocation and exhaustion, mirroring the emotional and physical toll of women’s daily lives. It slows down the pacing, forcing the reader to absorb the detailed list of burdens women face, thereby heightening the mood of frustration and weariness
 

Short Sentences

In contrast, Markov employs shorter, more impactful sentences to underscore key points or drive home criticisms: "И ако това е свобода, пожелавам я на тоя, който я е измислил!" This short, sharp sentence delivers a sarcastic punch, contrasting the earlier, more methodical descriptions of women's struggles. The brevity here injects a sense of immediacy and bluntness, which adds emotional weight to the statement. It punctuates the longer descriptions with a moment of biting clarity, making the critique of so-called "freedom" for women stand out. The pacing quickens, heightening the reader’s engagement and making the irony more pronounced.
 

Juxtaposition 

The alternation between long, detailed sentences and short, declarative ones creates a dynamic rhythm. The long sentences contribute to a slower, reflective pace that encourages the reader to contemplate the complexities of women's lives. These are often followed by shorter, more definitive sentences that break the reflective mood with a jarring, emphatic tone. This pattern builds tension and reinforces the critical tone of the passage, as the long sentences pull the reader into the depths of women's burdens, while the short sentences function as sharp, cutting conclusions to those reflections. The mixture of sentence lengths also helps develop a mood of growing frustration and disillusionment. The long sentences, with their detailed descriptions, evoke a sense of overwhelming pressure on women, while the shorter sentences often deliver a final, bitter conclusion that adds a sense of cynicism to the mood. The contrast between the two sentence structures mirrors the contrast between the facade of women’s liberation and the harsh reality they face.

For instance, when Markov uses a long sentence to list the gruelling daily tasks of women, it creates a rhythm that feels exhausting, mirroring the fatigue of the women themselves: "Аз съм свободна да ставам всяка сутрин в пет часа, да приготвя закуската, да нахраня детето, да взема трамвая в шест и половина, да го отнеса в детския дом, за да мога в осем без 15 да се явя на работа..." (I am free to get up every morning at five o'clock, prepare breakfast, feed the child, take the tram at six-thirty, take him to the children's home so that I can report to work at eight-thirty...") The breathless pace of this sentence mimics the relentless, nonstop nature of the workday. The reader is pulled into the monotony and intensity of these tasks, contributing to a mood of empathy and shared exhaustion. The piling up of actions without a pause mirrors the overwhelming weight of women’s responsibilities.

Following such long sentences, Markov often breaks the flow with shorter, more cutting statements that bring clarity and forcefulness to his critique: "И ако това е свобода, пожелавам я на тоя, който я е измислил!" (And if this is freedom, I wish it on the one who invented it!) This shift from the long, descriptive passages to short, direct sentences creates an abrupt contrast that reinforces the emotional punch of his commentary. The shorter sentences act as moments of sharp realization or judgment, intensifying the critical mood and providing a biting edge to the narrative. 

Figurative Language

The metaphors and imagery in the chapter reveal the narrator’s deep sense of frustration with the shallow nature of societal gestures toward women’s liberation. They emphasize themes of exploitation, disillusionment, and the contrast between appearance and reality, aligning with the broader critique of socialist Bulgaria’s treatment of women. These literary devices reflect the narrator’s internal experience of witnessing this hypocrisy and provide insight into the emotional and physical costs of the supposed emancipation women have achieved.

Behind the facade ("зад фасадата"): The title itself, "Зад фасадата на 8-и март" ("Behind the Facade of March 8th"), acts as an overarching metaphor. The word "фасада" (facade) suggests that the celebrations of International Women’s Day are merely a superficial cover, masking the deeper truths of women's suffering and exploitation. This metaphor conveys the idea that the public, outward displays of respect and recognition are hollow, obscuring the reality of ongoing inequality and hardship. It highlights the theme of hypocrisy and the gap between appearance and reality, which is central to the narrator’s critique. .

Imagery of exhaustion and burden: Throughout the passage, Markov uses vivid imagery to depict the overwhelming daily struggles of women. The description of a woman’s daily routine, such as "блъскам като добиче" ("I work like a beast of burden"), is a powerful simile that compares women’s labour to that of an animal, dehumanizing and mechanical. This comparison emphasizes the physical and emotional toll that the double burden of work and household responsibilities places on women. The imagery of hard labour, with its relentless, repetitive nature, reveals the narrator’s sympathy for the women’s plight and his critique of how this supposed "freedom" is actually a form of exploitation. 

The second paycheck ("втората заплата"): The "втората заплата" (second paycheck) metaphor represents more than just additional income. It symbolizes women’s contribution to the household, but at a steep cost. The metaphor of the paycheck stands for the illusion of economic independence while, in reality, it ties women even further into a system of labour and exhaustion. The narrator notes that the price of this second paycheck is "нечовешки висока" ("inhumanly high"), conveying that the pursuit of economic equality comes at the expense of personal well-being and family stability. This highlights the supposed gains of women’s liberation are undermined by the excessive burdens placed on them, leading to a new form of enslavement rather than freedom.

Dependence: A key metaphor in the passage is the replacement of dependence on husbands with dependence on society. The narrator reflects that "зависимостта от съпруга бе заменена от далеч по-издевателската зависимост от обществото" ("dependence on the husband was replaced by an even more oppressive dependence on society"). This metaphor underscores the theme of false freedom: women may have gained economic independence from their husbands, but they are still trapped in a system that exploits them. The metaphor of dependence highlights the illusory nature of emancipation in this context, where one form of control has merely been replaced by another, arguably harsher, form of subjugation.

Physical exploitation: Markov frequently uses imagery related to physical labour, particularly in his descriptions of women working in factories or performing traditionally male roles. He mentions women carrying bricks or working as miners, which creates stark, visual contrasts between societal expectations of femininity and the brutal reality of their labour. The imagery of women bent over in the fields or carrying heavy loads conveys a sense of suffering and endurance, underscoring the harsh conditions women face. This imagery serves to critique the state's failure to truly liberate women, as their physical strength is exploited in ways that contradict any notion of equality.

Public celebration: The celebratory imagery at the beginning of the passage, with festive decorations, an orchestra, and a buffet, contrasts sharply with the darker realities discussed later. This juxtaposition of a joyful, colourful setting with the bitter truths of women’s everyday struggles deepens the sense of irony in the passage. The imagery of the cheerful atmosphere masks the more sombre, unspoken experiences of the women, reinforcing the idea of a facade that conceals underlying suffering.

Theoretical insights

Post-Colonial Theory

While this chapter cannot be directly tied to colonialism, post-colonial theory’s focus on power dynamics, oppression, and subjugation can be applied to the societal structures Markov critiques in this and other chapters. In this case, the metaphorical "facade" of women’s emancipation under the socialist regime parallels post-colonial analyses of how dominant powers (whether imperial or governmental) impose narratives of liberation that conceal deeper systemic exploitation. The facade of International Women's Day celebrations can be viewed as a tool of hegemonic control, where the state constructs a narrative of progress and equality, yet beneath this surface, women remain subjugated by new forms of labour exploitation. Just as post-colonial theory critiques how colonizing powers "liberate" colonized people only to continue exerting control through economic or social means, Markov's passage critiques the state’s redefinition of women’s roles as supposedly liberating, while they remain economically and physically exploited. The economic independence offered to women is framed as liberation, but, in reality, it reflects an ongoing dependency on a larger system of state control, echoing how post-colonial subjects may be freed from direct rule but still trapped within neo-colonialstructures of dominance. 

Memory Studies

Markov's passage can be examined through the lens of memory studies, particularly in how collective and personal memories are constructed and contested. The passage highlights the tension between public, state-sponsored narratives of progress and the personal, often unspoken experiences of hardship and oppression. The idealized public memory of March 8th—celebrating women’s supposed liberation—clashes with the bitter personal memories of daily struggle described by the women. This disconnect between state-constructed memory and lived experience mirrors debates in memory studies about how collective memories are shaped, often by those in power, to enforce a particular narrative, while personal or marginalized memories may challenge these dominant frameworks. In this case, the state’s narrative of women's empowerment becomes a form of "memory politics," where the celebration of March 8th is intended to overwrite or obscure the harsher realities of life for women in Bulgaria. Markov’s critique opens a space for recovering these repressed memories, foregrounding the lived experiences of women that are excluded from the official memory of liberation and progress.

Affective Theory

Affective theory, with its focus on emotions, bodily responses, and how they shape and are shaped by societal structures, can provide a rich analysis of Markov's depiction of women’s experiences. The passage is infused with a sense of exhaustion, disillusionment, and irony, which are conveyed through both the women’s physical labour and the emotional toll of their daily lives. The repeated imagery of women performing backbreaking tasks creates a visceral sense of weariness, while the bitter sarcasm expressed in phrases like "И ако това е свобода, пожелавам я на тоя, който я е измислил!" ("And if this is freedom, I wish it upon whoever invented it!") captures a deeply affective response to the disconnect between the promises of socialism and the lived reality of exploitation. Affective theory helps us understand how emotions such as frustration, disillusionment, and bitterness are not just individual responses but are shaped by the broader socio-political context. The state’s rhetoric of liberation produces feelings of alienation and exhaustion in women, as their supposed freedom leads to more demanding labour and emotional strain. These affective experiences are integral to the narrator’s critique, as they reveal the embodied, emotional consequences of systemic oppression.

Narrative Voice

The narrator’s voice is highly subjective, presenting a deeply personal and critical perspective on the societal treatment of women in socialist Bulgaria. The narrator, presumed to be Markov himself, reflects on the hollow nature of the International Women’s Day celebrations and uses his personal observations to offer a broader critique of the systemic exploitation of women. His voice is imbued with irony, frustration, and disillusionment, making it clear that he does not view the narrative of women’s emancipation as genuine. This subjectivity shapes the reader’s understanding by framing the events of March 8th and the broader treatment of women as part of a larger, hypocritical system.

Although the narrator’s perspective is subjective, it does not necessarily make him unreliable. His personal involvement and critical stance provide a sharp lens through which to view the discrepancy between the state's claims of equality and the lived experiences of women. His observations, such as the working conditions of women and their dual burden of family and labour, are grounded in concrete examples. For instance, he vividly describes the gruelling daily routine of a woman who juggles work and household duties, offering an empathetic portrayal of her exhaustion and frustration. These detailed accounts lend credibility to his perspective, suggesting that while the narrator is critical and emotionally invested, he is also presenting a truthful reflection of the realities women face.

The narrative perspective, shaped by Markov’s critical tone and personal involvement, invites the reader to question the official state narrative of women’s liberation. By positioning himself as both an insider (who partakes in the superficial celebrations) and an outsider (who sees through their empty nature), the narrator directs the reader’s attention to the contradictions inherent in the socialist state’s rhetoric. This dual perspective encourages the reader to align with the narrator’s critical view, creating a sense of shared disillusionment with the system. Ultimately, the subjectivity of the narrator serves to deepen the critique of state hypocrisy and allows for a more nuanced understanding of the complexities and contradictions faced by women in this context.
